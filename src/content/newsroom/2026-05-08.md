# The Watchdog Citizens Are Already Among Us

*An essay from the Newsroom on civic recruitment - what the school-trust record tells us about who defends the trusts, and why parents, teachers, school-board members, journalists, and attentive citizens matter. May 8, 2026.*

---

The school-trust system has been running, in one state or another, for more than two centuries. Across that span the trusts have drifted, recovered, drifted again, been raided in some legislative sessions, and held their ground in others. The record has a single most-reliable predictor of whether a trust held or failed in any given decade. It is not only the wording of the federal land grant, the state constitution, or the management agency. It is whether a durable community of citizens - parents, teachers, school-board members, journalists, and people who simply chose to pay attention - was watching across the years when no one else was.

Margaret Bird, who has spent forty years inside the system as a state economist and a national authority on school-trust law, has put the matter in operating terms. *"Most people do not realize they are the beneficiaries of this trust,"* she has said. *"It is for their children and grandchildren. So a parent's role is to ask their school board and their legislators: How are our school trust lands being managed? How much money are we getting from them? A regular citizen can be a watchdog."*

That last word is the operative one. *Watchdog.* Margaret uses it deliberately. A school-trust watchdog is not a professional title or a partisan label. It is a civic role: the person who notices whether school-trust money is being managed for schools, asks plain questions in public, and keeps the record from disappearing into agency files.

The school-trust record makes this explicit. Utah's contemporary recovery, from a $50 million fund in 1983 to a multi-billion-dollar fund today, did not happen because Utah had better paper promises than its neighbors. It happened because Utah had, by the early 1990s, a coalition of parents, teachers, school boards, and attentive professionals who understood the math and would not let the legislature forget it. Oregon's century of slow drift did not happen because Oregon's constitution was weak; Margaret has described Oregon's school-trust language as among the strongest in the country. The drift happened because too few people were assigned, organized, and informed enough to insist that the promise be kept in daily practice.

Paper promises without a public constituency drift. Public concern without legal footing has too little leverage. School trusts need both.

## What Citizen Watchdogs Actually Do

Citizen watchdogs are not exotic. They are usually the people who happen to be in the room when an ordinary decision affecting school-trust money is being made.

It is the parent who sees a line item about the school's annual school-trust-lands disbursement and asks whether it matches what the state's distribution formula said it should. It is the teacher who notices that the School Community Council allocation has not arrived and asks why. It is the school-board member who reads the permanent-fund quarterly report carefully enough to notice that the management fee has crept up by a quarter of a percent. It is the journalist who covers the State Land Board's quarterly meeting because no one else does, and writes the four-paragraph item that, in two years, becomes the document somebody else's brief cites.

None of these roles requires heroism. Each requires presence - sustained, season-after-season presence in the rooms where the trust's ordinary administrative life happens. Margaret has been firm about the math: *"It only takes a few dedicated people to start making a real difference."*

The 2026 environment changes the cost of being one of those few. Work that, in 1986, would have required a state archivist's training can now often be started by a citizen with an internet connection, patience, and help finding public records. Research tools do not replace the citizen's judgment. They make it easier for ordinary people to ask better questions and check the answers.

Margaret has also been firm about how a citizen constituency, in any state, actually gets built. In her words: *"Increase the revenue to schools, get it directly to every single school where the parents, the teachers, and the principal are deciding how the money is implemented. And all of a sudden, you have built a huge constituency."* She offers Utah as evidence of scale. Roughly 9,000 Utahns sit on school community councils that help govern per-school distributions from the Utah School LAND Trust Program. Each of them has a reason to know what the trust is doing. The word spreads because the money reaches a room where parents and educators are already making school decisions.

Margaret led the Utah reform from a $5 million annual program to a $150 million annual program - thirty times the original amount - and she is explicit that the legal structure and the public constituency grew together. Give people a real school-level reason to pay attention, and they will defend the thing they understand.

## How to Begin

For Oregon readers, the first step is simple: learn what the Common School Fund and school-trust lands are supposed to do, then ask whether today's management choices are serving schools and students.

Useful starting points:

- Read the OASTL briefing materials on Oregon's Common School Fund, the Elliott State Forest, and the current cases.

- Ask your school board, district, or legislator how school-trust revenue is being tracked and explained to local schools.

- Help keep public records findable: meeting minutes, court filings, budget documents, and state reports matter only if people can locate and understand them.

- Join, donate, or share the work with parents, teachers, school-board members, reporters, and lawyers who should know this issue exists.

The people who designed America's school land grants built many safeguards into law. What they could not build by themselves was a living public memory that lasts across generations. That part has to be rebuilt by citizens.

The watchdog citizens are already among us. The question is whether they choose, this year, to occupy the office.
